"We do both B2B and B2C" often gets read as "we don't have a point of view." In reality, **B2B and B2C web design** demand genuinely different decisions — not because the technology changes, but because the *buyer* does.

Get the psychology right and the same underlying craft produces two very different, equally effective websites. Get it wrong and you end up with a B2B site that reads like a consumer store, or a consumer store that reads like a procurement document — both of which convert badly for reasons the design review will never surface.

Here's exactly what changes between B2B website design and B2C website design, and how to design each one to convert.

## Different buyers, different clocks

A B2C shopper often decides in a single session, driven by desire and momentum. A B2B buyer decides over weeks, in committee, with a budget to justify. That one difference cascades into everything.

- **B2C web design** rewards *speed to desire*: strong imagery, social proof, and a frictionless path to purchase.
- **B2B web design** rewards *speed to confidence*: clear positioning, credible proof, and content that helps a champion sell you internally.

One design optimises for an individual acting now. The other optimises for a group deciding later. Everything else — UX, content depth, and calls to action — follows from that single difference.

## The person on the page isn't always the person paying

This is the structural fact that most B2B websites ignore, and it changes everything about how the page should be written.

In B2C, the visitor is usually the decision-maker, the budget holder, and the user, all in one. You persuade one person and the sale happens.

In B2B, the person reading your site is very often none of those things. They're a manager researching options who will have to convince a director, who will have to justify it to finance. Gartner's research puts the typical B2B buying group at [six to ten decision-makers, with buyers spending just 17% of the purchase journey meeting suppliers at all](https://www.gartner.com/en/sales/insights/b2b-buying-journey) — and that 17% is split across every vendor they're considering.

Read that number again, because it reframes the job entirely. The overwhelming majority of the decision happens in rooms you're not in, using materials you didn't present. Your website is not closing a deal — it's **arming an advocate for a meeting you will never attend.**

Design accordingly. That means the page needs things a consumer site would never bother with: a case study they can forward, a pricing structure they can defend, a one-line answer to "why not the cheaper option?" The most valuable thing your B2B site can do is make your champion look smart for suggesting you.

## What changes on the page

### The hero section

For B2C, the hero sells a feeling — the product in its best light with a clear price and an add-to-cart. For B2B, the hero sells clarity: *who you help*, *what outcome you deliver*, and a low-commitment next step like "Book a demo." Either way, a [high-converting hero](/blog/homepage-conversion) states the outcome first.

The sharpest difference is what you withhold. B2C hides nothing — price, product, delivery, all immediately visible, because friction is the enemy. B2B is usually explaining something the visitor doesn't yet have a mental category for, so the hero's job is to make them recognise their own problem in your words before you say anything about your solution.

### The proof

B2C proof is emotional and voluminous: star ratings, review counts, user photos. Quantity matters, because a consumer is asking "do people like me buy this, and were they happy?"

B2B proof is specific and authoritative: named case studies, measurable results, and logos your buyer recognises. Quantity matters far less than *relevance* — one detailed case study from a company that looks like theirs beats two hundred anonymous five-star ratings, because the B2B buyer is asking a different question: "has this worked for someone with my exact problem, at my size, in my industry?"

### The call to action

> B2C asks for a decision. B2B asks for a conversation.

A "Buy now" button and a "Talk to sales" button are not the same UI problem. One removes friction from a transaction; the other lowers the risk of starting a relationship.

That's why B2B sites need a *ladder* of commitment rather than a single button. Not everyone is ready to talk to sales, and if that's the only option you've offered, everyone who isn't ready simply leaves and you never learn they existed. Give them a rung they can reach: a guide, a pricing page, a case study, a self-serve calculator.

### The content depth

A consumer buying a lamp does not want a 2,000-word article about lamps. A procurement manager evaluating a supplier genuinely does want the detail — they're building a case, and every unanswered question is a reason for someone senior to say no.

This is why B2B sites carry documentation, comparison pages, and long-form content that would be dead weight on a consumer store. It isn't padding. It's ammunition.

### The pricing question

B2C shows the price. Always. A consumer who can't find a price assumes the worst and leaves.

B2B is the genuinely hard call. "Contact us for pricing" protects your negotiating position and filters out tyre-kickers — but it also loses every buyer who was quietly building a shortlist and needed a number to include you. The middle path usually wins: publish a starting point, a range, or the *shape* of your pricing ("projects typically start from X, scoped to your requirements"). Give them enough to defend you internally without committing you to a figure.

## What B2B and B2C web design share

It's tempting to think B2B can be duller because the audience is "professional." It can't. The person reading your B2B website is the same human who shopped on a beautifully designed store the night before, and their expectations carry over. Nobody switches to a lower standard of taste when they arrive at the office.

Both audiences demand the fundamentals of good web design:

1. A page that feels effortless and is built around how customers actually buy.
2. A clear, jargon-free promise.
3. Responsive, mobile-first design that isn't an afterthought.
4. Obvious, trustworthy next steps.
5. Clean, SEO-optimised structure so the right people can find you — get this wrong and [you simply won't show up on Google](/blog/website-not-showing-on-google), whoever you sell to.
6. Speed. A [slow site](/blog/website-speed-core-web-vitals) loses a consumer's impulse and a business buyer's patience equally.

And both are judged on credibility within milliseconds of arriving, long before anyone reads a word — which is why the [small design details that build trust](/blog/design-details-trust) matter identically on both sides.

## The mobile assumption that catches B2B out

There's a persistent belief in B2B that mobile doesn't matter because "our buyers are at their desks." It's usually wrong, and it's an expensive thing to be wrong about.

Business buyers do the *research* on their phones — in the evening, between meetings, on the train. They may well fill in your form on a laptop later, but if the mobile experience was bad, there is no later. The desk visit you're designing for only happens if the phone visit went well.

Google settles the argument anyway: it uses [mobile-first indexing](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/mobile/mobile-sites-mobile-first-indexing), meaning the mobile version of your site is the one it crawls, indexes, and ranks. If your mobile experience is an afterthought, it isn't just your buyers who see the worse version — it's Google, and it's the only version Google sees.

## Which one does your business need?

Most growing companies actually need both — a lead-generating B2B site *and* conversion-focused B2C pages — built on one consistent brand system. That's exactly the kind of [B2B and B2C website design](/#services) we specialise in.

If you genuinely serve both, resist the urge to average them into one page that vaguely addresses everyone. Split the paths early — distinct entry points, distinct proof, distinct calls to action — on a shared foundation of brand, speed, and craft. A homepage that tries to speak to a consumer and a procurement manager in the same sentence persuades neither.

## Common questions

### Is B2B web design harder than B2C?

Different, not harder. B2C is brutally unforgiving on execution — a half-second of delay or a clumsy checkout costs you sales immediately and measurably. B2B is unforgiving on *clarity*, and punishes you invisibly: you never find out about the buyer who couldn't work out what you do, or who couldn't defend your price to their boss.

### Should a B2B website show pricing?

Show something. Full transparency isn't always possible, but "Contact us" as the only answer loses buyers who are building a shortlist and need a figure to justify including you. A starting price, a range, or a clear description of what drives the cost keeps you in the running without locking you into a number. Our own [pricing guide](/blog/website-cost-malaysia) takes exactly that approach, and it's the one we'd recommend.

### Do B2B websites still need to look good?

Yes, and the "our buyers only care about substance" argument is usually a rationalisation for a dated site. Credibility is assessed visually and almost instantly, and a B2B buyer carrying six-figure risk is *more* sensitive to signals of carelessness, not less. Substance is the price of entry. Looking like you have it is how you get read at all.

### Can one website serve both B2B and B2C customers?

Yes, but not by blending them. Give each audience its own path from the homepage down, with proof and calls to action tuned to how that buyer actually decides, on a shared foundation of brand, performance, and design system. The mistake is a single averaged page that speaks to both — it lands as generic, which is the one thing neither audience forgives.

### What's the most common B2B web design mistake?

Writing for yourself instead of your buyer. B2B sites are full of company history, values, and internal vocabulary, and almost empty of the thing the visitor came for: a plain statement of what problem you solve and whether you've solved it for someone like them.

The craft — performance, accessibility, hierarchy, restraint — is universal. What changes is *where you spend the attention you earn*. Design for the buyer's clock, not the industry label, and the B2B-versus-B2C debate mostly dissolves.
