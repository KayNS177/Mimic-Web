Almost every business that asks us to quote a website has already been told, by someone, that they should "just use WordPress." Often by a developer. Sometimes by a friend. Occasionally by an agency that only builds WordPress sites, which is a bit like asking a barber whether you need a haircut.

WordPress runs a huge share of the web — [somewhere north of 40% of all sites](https://w3techs.com/technologies/details/cm-wordpress), by most counts. That is not an accident, and it is not a scam. It's a genuinely good answer to a genuinely common problem.

It's just not the answer to *every* problem, and the sales pitch on both sides is unusually dishonest. So here is the comparison as we'd give it to a friend, including the parts that don't favour us.

## The short version

- **Publishing a lot of content, on a small budget, with non-technical staff editing daily?** WordPress, and don't overthink it.
- **A marketing site where speed, design, and conversion decide whether you get the lead?** Custom.
- **A site that has to *do* things — book, quote, calculate, sync with your systems?** Custom, and the website is really software at that point.
- **Genuinely unsure?** Start with the section on how to decide, at the bottom. It's four questions.

Everything below is the reasoning behind those four lines.

## First, what "custom" actually means

There's a caricature of custom websites — a lone developer hand-writing every page in a text editor, and you ringing them up every time you want to change a phone number. If that's what you're being sold, walk away.

A modern custom build means the *site* is built for you, not that the *tooling* is invented from scratch. It's assembled from mature, open, extremely well-tested foundations — the same rendering libraries and hosting platforms that large product teams use — with your pages, your design, and your logic on top. Content can still live in a CMS your team edits without touching code.

So the real comparison isn't "flexible platform vs fragile bespoke thing." It's: **do you want a general-purpose system adapted to fit you, or a system shaped around you from the start?** Both are legitimate. They just fail in different directions.

## Where WordPress genuinely wins

Let's be fair to it, because a lot of "custom is always better" content isn't.

**Content velocity.** If your business publishes constantly — a news operation, a busy blog, a magazine — WordPress is superb. The editing experience is mature, everyone has used it, and you can hire someone who knows it in an afternoon.

**Cheap to start.** A theme, a host, a few plugins, and you are live. For a business that needs *a* website more than it needs *the right* website, that is a completely rational trade.

**The ecosystem.** Whatever you want to bolt on, someone has built a plugin for it. Events, memberships, multilingual, forms, e-commerce. You will rarely hear "that's not possible."

**Hiring.** If we vanished tomorrow, you could replace a WordPress developer by Friday. That's a real risk-management argument and we won't pretend otherwise.

If several of those describe you, stop reading and go build a WordPress site. You'll be fine, and you'll save money.

## Where it starts costing you

The trouble with WordPress rarely shows up on day one. It shows up in month fourteen.

### The plugin tax

Every plugin is someone else's code running on your site, loading its own scripts and styles on every page — including the pages that don't use it. A contact form plugin loads on your blog posts. A slider loads on your contact page.

Twenty plugins in, the site is carrying a lot of weight nobody chose deliberately. Each one was a sensible decision in isolation. Together they're the reason the homepage takes four seconds to become usable.

That matters commercially, not just aesthetically, because [Google measures how the page actually feels to real visitors](/blog/website-speed-core-web-vitals) — how fast the main content appears, how quickly it responds to a tap, whether it jumps around while you're reading. Those measurements feed ranking, and they're the first thing a plugin-heavy site loses.

You can absolutely make a WordPress site fast. It just requires actively fighting the default, forever, and most sites lose that fight quietly.

### Maintenance you didn't budget for

Plugins need updating. Updates occasionally break each other. Somebody has to notice, and somebody has to fix it.

And because WordPress is on such a vast number of sites, it is a permanent target — though it's worth being precise about *what* gets attacked. The core software is well maintained by a serious security team. The overwhelming majority of real-world compromises come in through third-party plugins and themes, which is exactly what you can check for yourself in a [public vulnerability database like WPScan's](https://wpscan.com/). The attack surface is the stuff you bolted on.

The honest version: WordPress isn't insecure. *Unmaintained* WordPress is insecure, and a large number of small-business WordPress sites are, functionally, unmaintained — which is how a brochure site for a plumber ends up quietly serving spam.

### The design ceiling

Themes are built to sell to thousands of buyers, so they're built to be adaptable, which means they're built to be generic. You can push a theme a long way, but you are always negotiating with someone else's decisions, and the compromises accumulate in exactly the places that decide whether a visitor trusts you — [the small details of type, spacing, and polish that read as credibility](/blog/design-details-trust) before anyone consciously evaluates anything.

This is the single most common reason businesses come to us for a rebuild: not that the WordPress site is broken, but that it looks like a WordPress site, and they're pitching against companies that don't.

## The SEO question, settled

Let's kill a myth in both directions.

**WordPress does not rank better than a custom site, and a custom site does not rank better than WordPress.** Google does not know or care what you built with. It cares whether it can crawl your pages, whether the content answers the query, and whether the page is a decent experience.

What *is* true is that the two approaches make different mistakes easy:

- WordPress hands you excellent SEO plumbing via plugins, then makes it easy to bury under a slow, bloated theme.
- A custom build gives you total control of the technical foundations, then makes it easy to ship a beautiful site where a developer forgot the meta descriptions, or where the content only appears after JavaScript runs and Google indexes an empty shell.

Both failure modes are real, and we've seen both. If your site isn't showing up, the cause is [almost never the platform](/blog/website-not-showing-on-google) — it's an indexing problem or a content problem hiding behind the platform argument.

> Nobody has ever lost a ranking for choosing the wrong CMS. Plenty have lost one by shipping a slow page nobody links to.

## The cost comparison nobody runs properly

WordPress is cheaper to build. It is not automatically cheaper to *own*, and the comparison people run — build cost vs build cost — is the wrong one.

Run it over three years instead, and count: hosting, premium plugin licences (most of the good ones are annual), the maintenance retainer or the hours someone on your team spends on updates, the developer you call when an update breaks checkout, and the rebuild you'll do sooner because the theme aged.

Sometimes WordPress still wins that maths comfortably. Sometimes the "cheap" site turns out to have cost more than the expensive one, which is why we wrote a whole [breakdown of what websites actually cost](/blog/website-cost), including the ongoing line items nobody quotes you.

The point isn't that custom is cheaper. Often it isn't. The point is that build price is not price.

## The question that actually decides it

Here's the one that settles most real cases, and it has nothing to do with budget:

**Does your website need to *do* something, or just *say* something?**

If it needs to say something — who you are, what you sell, why you're credible, how to contact you — then it's a marketing site, and both options are on the table.

But the moment it needs to *do* things — take a booking against live availability, generate a quote from a customer's inputs, show a client their order status, talk to your inventory or CRM — you have crossed a line. You are no longer buying a website. You are commissioning [software that happens to have a marketing site attached](/blog/website-that-works-like-software).

You can force that through WordPress with enough plugins. People do it every day. But you end up maintaining a stack of third-party extensions, glued together, each of which can break the others on update — and the "cheap" option has quietly become the expensive, fragile one. That's the same trade-off, in a different costume, as choosing [off-the-shelf tools over software built around your workflow](/blog/custom-software-vs-off-the-shelf).

## How to decide, in four questions

1. **How often does your content change?** Daily → WordPress's editing experience earns its keep. A few times a year → that advantage is nearly worthless to you.
2. **Does the site need to do things, or say things?** Do → custom. Say → keep going.
3. **Is design a competitive weapon in your market?** If you're pitching against slicker-looking rivals, a generic theme is an active liability. If you're the only supplier in town, it genuinely doesn't matter.
4. **Who maintains it in year two?** Be honest. If the answer is "nobody, really," pick the option with the smallest maintenance surface — and that is usually not twenty plugins.

If you answered "daily / say / no / we have someone," build it on WordPress and spend the savings on marketing. That's a real recommendation, not a rhetorical device.

## Common questions

### Is WordPress bad for SEO?

No. WordPress is perfectly capable of ranking, and plugins give you solid SEO fundamentals out of the box. What hurts rankings is what tends to *accompany* a typical WordPress build: a heavy theme, twenty plugins loading scripts on every page, and the resulting slow, unstable page experience. The platform isn't the problem; the accumulated weight is. A lean WordPress site will out-rank a bloated custom one every time.

### Is a custom website more expensive than WordPress?

To build, almost always yes. To own over three years, not necessarily. WordPress carries ongoing costs that rarely appear in the initial comparison — premium plugin licences, maintenance time, the developer you call when an update breaks something, and an earlier rebuild as the theme ages. Compare three-year totals rather than build quotes, and the gap narrows sharply. Sometimes it reverses.

### Can I still edit my own content on a custom site?

Yes, and this is the most common misconception we run into. "Custom" refers to the site being built for you, not to content being locked away from you. A custom build can include a CMS your team edits without touching code — the difference is that it's shaped around the content you actually publish, rather than a general-purpose editor you have to bend into shape.

### Should I migrate my existing WordPress site?

Not on principle, and not because someone told you WordPress is old-fashioned. Migrate when there's a concrete reason: the site is slow and the theme fights every attempt to fix it, maintenance and licences are costing more than they should, the design is losing you deals, or you need functionality that plugins can only fake. If none of those are true, your WordPress site is doing its job and the money is better spent elsewhere.

### What about Wix, Squarespace, or Webflow?

They're reasonable, and the same logic applies. Wix and Squarespace are the "publish something decent quickly" option — excellent for very small sites, limiting once you need real control over speed and structure. Webflow sits in the middle, with genuinely good design freedom and a real ceiling once your site needs custom logic. The four questions above sort them just as well as they sort WordPress.
